### The Story of Mala Beads

#### Mala, originates from the Sanskrit language of ancient India/Nepal, and translates to “garland” in English. The word bead comes from “bede” an anglo-saxon term meaning to pray. The Mala beads are a religious material object traditionally stemming from Buddihism and hinduism. Its main purpose was used for prayer/meditation purposes such as Japa in the method of saying a mantra. Though today the use of mala prayer beads have been popularly spread across continents and different cultures. To define the terminology used, japa is a sanskrit word which means to “utter in a low voice repeat internally” its a meditative repetition of a mantra. Japa is a practice found in buddhism, hinudism, janism, and sikhism. A mantra is a group of words, syllable or sound repeated to aid concentration in meditation. Some mantras are ancient sanskrit words believed to have spiritual energy others mantras can be said to have no literal meaning at all.

#### A mala has 109 beads, 108 are for chanting and the head bead, a sumeru, which is used to keep track of rounds of chanting. After every complete round of chanting, the direction of  the beads is reversed. One round of chanting covers all 108 beads, beginning with the bead on one side of the sumeru and ending with the bead on the other side of the sumeru. The book, "Beads of Intent", by Nicholas Breeze Wood details the use of mala beads in its practice and the ritual context associated: 
> "The beads of a mala are moved one bead at a time, each time the mantra is said, and over time the beads become worn byt the movement of fingers, making an old mala an aspect of spiritual and physical beauty. It also builds up the intent of the power of the practice for the practitioner and the mala becomes a powerful object in their practice. They can be made with a combination of natural gemstones, crystal, pearls, wood or seed beads. A tassel is attached to the beads, with talismans or amulets depending on personal preference or tradition." (Wood) 

#### Mala beads are said to originate from a story written in early texts known as rthe Mokugenji Sutra. The story is about a king named Haruri who was suggested by the Buddha to craft a strong of beads from seeds and use it to recite the mantra by counting each bead. The reasoning for the number is beads was specific from mokugenji sutra which denoted the number of 108 beads in the original mala. The number 108 is significant in both the hinduism and buddhism but there’s a difference in its meanings. For hinduism it relays the cosmic significance of the number itself (108 is the basis of creation, a number that represents units of distance between our body and God within us) while buddhism refers to the number of passions to which one is striving to “put an end”. Mala beads today can be used for multiple purposes today such as provide grounding while meditating, yoga practice, worry beads for anxiety, healing, grief, self-love, setting postive affirmations and gemstone energy for balancing the chakra.
